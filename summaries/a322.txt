David Ogilvy (The King of Madison Avenue)

A Formidable Character Shaped by Early Hardship

• Ogilvy modeled himself after his grandfather — "hard as nails" and self-made — rather than his father, who lost his fortune and attempted suicide, leaving the family in poverty.
• Humiliating experiences in school (being publicly shamed for being on scholarship) and dropping out of Oxford without a degree fueled a lifelong, obsessive drive to achieve success and wealth.
• He described himself as a misfit and rebel in school, later returning to declare: "There is no correlation between success at school and success in life."
• His core identity was built not through formal education but through relentless self-directed reading, primarily biographies of successful leaders in business and government.

An Eclectic Pre-Advertising Education That Built His Foundation

• Before founding his agency, Ogilvy worked as a chef in Paris, a door-to-door salesman, a researcher at the Gallup Poll, and a British intelligence officer — each role sharpening his understanding of human behavior, persuasion, and standards of excellence.
• As a 24-year-old Aga stove salesman, he wrote a sales manual Fortune Magazine called "probably the best sales manual ever written" — using it to land his first advertising job.
• His time in the French kitchen under Chef Pitard instilled his lifelong standard: "What is not perfect is bad" — and the belief that high standards make people feel they're part of the best team in the world.
• A brief stint living among the Amish (preferring horses, candlelight, and handwritten notes) showed his deeply eccentric, contrarian personality that ran throughout his career.

Mentors and the Power of Copying the Best

• Ogilvy deliberately sought out masters of his craft — Rosser Reeves, Claude Hopkins, and John Caples — crediting them as the primary sources of his advertising philosophy, which he maintained "substantially unchanged" throughout his career.
• He openly admitted to plagiarizing Caples, stating at his eulogy: "Why steal from anyone but the best?" — viewing the adoption of proven ideas as a strategic virtue, not a weakness.
• Hopkins' Scientific Advertising so influenced Ogilvy that he declared no one should work in advertising until they had read it seven times.
• His philosophy — advertising must sell, not entertain; lead with a benefit headline; pack copy with facts — was learned from mentors and applied with relentless consistency for 50 years.

Building a Culture of Missionaries, Not Mercenaries

• Ogilvy founded his agency in 1948 at age 38 with $5,000 in savings and almost no direct advertising experience, yet set an audacious goal: win Shell, Lever Brothers, Campbell Soup, General Foods, and Bristol-Myers — and eventually won all five.
• He built a distinct agency culture where employees felt they had a mission, not just a job — modeled on the French kitchen ethos that high standards make people proud of their work.
• He networked with the founders of McKinsey, Arthur Andersen, and Goldman Sachs, comparing notes on building elite professional service firms; the lesson: everyone from the boardroom to the mailroom must understand the firm's values.
• He famously walked out of a pitch when told a 12-person committee would approve all advertising decisions — refusing to let committees dilute creative work.

Mastery of Writing and the Dogma of Brevity

• Ogilvy became one of advertising's greatest writers by writing constantly — letters to his brother several times a week, memos so voluminous that his local French post office was reclassified to handle the volume.
• He believed in the "dogma of brevity" — using aphorisms, vivid metaphors, and compressed language to make ideas memorable: "Pay peanuts, get monkeys" and "You can't save souls in an empty church."
• His ads were meticulously formatted — double-spaced, short paragraphs, underlined key phrases — and often loaded with facts, based on the principle: "The more you tell, the more you sell."
• His Rolls-Royce headline ("At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock") was pulled from a 20-year-old article after three weeks of research — illustrating that brilliance is often the product of preparation, not inspiration.

Late-Life Regrets and an Enduring Legacy

• Ogilvy sold most of his company stock out of persistent financial fear, then watched helplessly as Ogilvy & Mather was taken over for ~$862 million — a hostile acquisition he called the destruction of the most important thing in his life.
• His financial mismanagement (living in a French château he couldn't afford, poor investment decisions) left him without the wealth his success should have secured: "I'm supposed to be terribly rich. I'm not because I screwed up my financial affairs all my life."
• In later years, he openly listed regrets: turning down Xerox, giving up creative work for management, wasting time on unimportant things, and failing to recognize big ideas when they arrived.
• His epitaph, drawn from an old Scottish saying, captured his philosophy on life: "Be happy while you're living, for you're a long time dead."

Why Leonardo was a saboteur, Gutenberg went broke, and Florence was weird – Ada Palmer

Why Italian City-States Became Republics

• After Rome's collapse, wealthier cities with strong agricultural land could self-govern by forming councils and senates; weaker towns collapsed into feudal lordship structures centered around noble villas.
• Italy's agricultural richness enabled more cities to sustain themselves as self-governing republics, whereas elsewhere in Europe power consolidated into monarchies.
• Florence specifically leveraged cultural capital — classical architecture, Greek scholarship, and Platonic philosophy — to punch above its social weight, using Renaissance aesthetics as diplomatic and political currency.
• The Medici, despite being "merchant scum" by aristocratic standards, used the trappings of Roman antiquity to gain legitimacy and forge alliances that pure military power couldn't achieve.

The Petrarchan Dream and Machiavelli's Correction

• Petrarch, traumatized by the Black Death and pervasive civil war, proposed that studying Roman virtue (Cicero, Livy, Plato) could produce better leaders through a kind of moral osmosis — surround rulers with great texts and they'll become great men.
• This idea was co-opted propagandistically: upstart tyrants dressed in Roman aesthetics to confer legitimacy upon themselves, not out of genuine civic virtue.
• The experiment failed spectacularly — Cesare Borgia had all the classical education and still "set fire to half the world."
• Machiavelli responded by proposing what we'd now call political science: treating history as a casebook of tactical decisions, studying what worked and what didn't, rather than absorbing classical virtue through osmosis.

Leonardo and Brunelleschi as "Saboteurs" of Progress

• Being a scientist, in Ada Palmer's framing, requires publishing and sharing results with a community so that collective knowledge advances — Leonardo did the opposite, writing everything in coded mirror script to preserve his uniqueness.
• Leonardo and Brunelleschi (who burned his dome schematics) were brilliant inventors who deliberately suppressed their discoveries to remain irreplaceable masters, much like the ancients they revered.
• Francis Bacon's bee metaphor captures the paradigm shift around 1600: the ideal knowledge-worker should combine observation of the world (the ant) with theoretical synthesis (the spider) and produce something genuinely new — shared honey.
• The modern scientific community — publishing, peer review, collaborative progress — was a deliberate cultural invention of the early 1600s, not a natural evolution.

Gutenberg's Bankruptcy and the Print Revolution's Slow Burn

• Gutenberg went bankrupt because he solved production without solving distribution — printing 300 Bibles was useless when only 7 people in his town could legally own one.
• Economic sustainability came only when printers moved to Venice (a Mediterranean shipping hub), enabling copies to travel on outgoing ships to diverse markets; book fairs like Frankfurt's then formalized distribution.
• The printing press didn't transform Europe in a single moment — it took 40 years to become profitable, then successively spawned pamphlets (enabling the Reformation), newspapers, and magazines (invented to fact-check newspapers), each wave comparable to a separate digital revolution.
• Palmer argues this mirrors our digital era: the computer, internet, social media, and LLMs are not separate revolutions but successive waves of one information revolution — unfolding on a similar 150-year timescale.

Writing Materials and the Economics of Knowledge

• Europe's post-Roman knowledge famine was partly a material crisis: papyrus rotted, and parchment (essentially leather) was so expensive that monks copying manuscripts had to choose which 100 of 1,000 texts to save — a process that inadvertently censored pagan antiquity in favor of Christian authors like Augustine.
• Paper arrived in Europe around 800 AD but was trusted so slowly that state documents weren't written on it for another 400 years; even after Gutenberg, wealthy patrons still ordered books printed on vellum.
• Gutenberg's bankruptcy was partly a paper-cost problem: he borrowed the equivalent of ~$1.5M to buy paper up front before selling a single copy.
• Pamphlets emerged as a cash-flow solution — a short, cheap print run that could be sold in days while a longer book took months to produce, directly funding the broader print economy.

The Inquisition's Blind Spots and Censorship's Chronic Misfocus

• Authorities consistently censored the wrong things — inquisitors in the Enlightenment obsessed over minor Jansenist theological disputes while largely ignoring Voltaire and Diderot's Encyclopédie, which actually reshaped Western thought.
• Of the thousands killed by the Inquisition, only one was executed for anything science-related; there were only 12 total scientific trials, versus hundreds of thousands of executions for religious non-conformity and colonial "paganism."
• Galileo's trial prompted the Inquisition to develop what amounted to peer review — building their own experimental laboratory to verify the science in books they were meant to ban, inadvertently advancing the very knowledge they sought to suppress.
• Milton's Areopagitica (the first major defense of press freedom) failed to prevent censorship; Paradise Lost was published under that same censorship regime, with censors objecting to one line about astrology while leaving Satan's anti-monarchical rhetoric untouched — a perfect illustration of how censors always misidentify the dangerous idea.

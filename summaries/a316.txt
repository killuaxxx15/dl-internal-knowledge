Alfred Nobel

A Paradoxical, Prickly Personality

• Nobel described himself as "a misanthrope and yet utterly benevolent," loathing publicity and deflecting praise with genuine discomfort.
• He was brilliant in private conversation — intellectually dazzling and stimulating — yet widely considered an unpleasant, demanding taskmaster.
• Work was his escape from melancholy; long 15–20 hour days were routine, and letters were his primary mode of communication.

Early Life: Poverty, Education, and a Father's Failures

• Nobel's father, Immanuel, was a recurring boom-and-bust entrepreneur who went bankrupt twice, leaving Alfred haunted by the humiliation of poverty throughout his life.
• Alfred was a sickly child but distinguished himself early through brains, discipline, and enterprise — traits his father identified in him at age 8.
• His greatest early lesson: financing a project is primary, not secondary — a direct rebuke of his father's and brother's financial recklessness.

The Invention of Dynamite and Building an Empire

• Nobel's discovery that nitroglycerin could be reliably detonated was the turning point of his life, leading to the invention of dynamite and a global explosives industry.
• Starting with less than $25,000 in capital and a covered barge to circumvent regulations, he bootstrapped the company himself — serving as inventor, manager, financier, and publicist.
• He ultimately built a multinational empire of factories, earned the equivalent of $110,000 per day at his peak, and held influence over the Nobel oil company and Bofors armaments company.

Financial Discipline and Independence

• Nobel obsessively tracked every expense — from a $6 hat to a $2.3 million loan to his brother — long after he could never spend his fortune.
• He consistently rejected business deals that would compromise his independence, refusing to let investors claim control over his future patents.
• He advised his brother: "Never do yourself what others could do better or equally well" — though at the start, he had no choice but to do everything himself.

Wealth Without Happiness: The Cautionary Tale

• Despite extraordinary success, Nobel experienced what biographers believe was clinical depression; his letters are filled with loneliness, complaints, and existential despair.
• He craved intimate connection but repelled people through his caustic personality, ending up isolated with few real friends in old age.
• His life serves as a stark cautionary tale: money did not buy happiness, and his failure to invest in relationships was his most consequential personal mistake.

The Nobel Prize: Reputation, Regret, and Legacy

• When his brother Ludvig died, newspapers mistakenly published Alfred's obituary, calling him a "merchant of death." The experience shocked and obsessed him.
• In response, Nobel rewrote his will, bequeathing the bulk of his vast fortune to establish the Nobel Prizes — awarded regardless of ideology, race, sex, or nationality.
• His prizes became an enduring monument: every December 10th, the world witnesses the legacy of a brilliant, deeply flawed man who chose to be remembered for peace rather than destruction.

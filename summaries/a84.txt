The Triumph of William McKinley: Why the Election of 1896 Still Matters Book Summary

William McKinley's Formative Years and Character

• William McKinley was a principled, ambitious man whose ambition was driven by his convictions and deep faith.
• He served with distinction in the Civil War, rising through the ranks from private to brevet major, an experience that connected him intimately with Americans from all walks of life.
• Following the war, McKinley became a lawyer, known for his diligent study habits, persuasive speaking, and ability to engender trust.

Early Political Career and Key Lessons Learned

• McKinley's political "apprenticeship" involved hard work, becoming well-versed in issues, and honing his oratorical skills.
• He learned the importance of perseverance, particularly in his unwavering fight to protect black voting rights.
• Tactical lessons included not sharing his speeches in advance after one was stolen and recognizing the critical role of efficient political organization.

McKinley's Advocacy for Protectionist Tariffs

• McKinley became a leading voice for protective tariffs, a central and passionate political issue in the Gilded Age that affected job creation and economic expansion.
• He argued that high tariffs benefited American workers and consumers by maintaining decent wages and fostering domestic competition.
• While he supported high duties on value-added goods, he was wary of significant tariff adjustments, calling them "delicate and hazardous".

The 1888 Republican National Convention and Loyalty

• McKinley gained national prominence through his leadership on tariffs and his impressive handling of the Resolutions Committee at the 1888 GOP Convention.
• Despite widespread delegate support and pressure from party bosses to accept the presidential nomination, McKinley firmly refused, upholding his pledged loyalty to John Sherman.
• His selflessness and adherence to personal honour during the convention impressed many, including Mark Hanna, who saw McKinley's future as a significant national power.

Strategy for the 1896 Presidential Primary Campaign

• McKinley's 1890 congressional defeat unexpectedly bolstered his stature as a political martyr, freeing him to pursue the presidency with Mark Hanna's encouragement.
• Their meticulous strategy focused on unifying Ohio Republicans and building a national campaign to secure an absolute majority of "instructed" delegates by avoiding political bargains and appealing to the grassroots.
• McKinley campaigned extensively from 1892-1894, making over 140 speeches and covering most of Ohio's counties, which showcased his popularity and secured early endorsements.

Confronting the "Combine" and Internal Party Challenges

• McKinley faced significant opposition from a "Combine" of Republican bosses, led by Thomas Collier Platt and Matthew S. Quay, who sought to prevent his nomination.
• The Combine attempted to undermine McKinley's image, particularly by portraying him as unreliable or evasive on the crucial currency issue.
• Despite these efforts, McKinley's superior organization and strategic focus on credentials fights allowed his forces to consistently prevail, demonstrating his campaign's methodical approach.

McKinley's Triumph at the 1896 Republican National Convention

• At the St. Louis convention, McKinley's team, expertly managed by Mark Hanna, effectively controlled the RNC and the credentials process, ensuring favorable outcomes for McKinley's delegates.
• While McKinley initially preferred to straddle the currency issue, Hanna and other leaders ultimately pushed for a clear gold standard plank, which McKinley reluctantly endorsed.
• McKinley secured the Republican presidential nomination on the first ballot, marking a decisive victory for his organized campaign and its principled stand on sound money.

The Rise of William Jennings Bryan and the "Cross of Gold"

• William Jennings Bryan, a young Nebraska Democrat, emerged as the leading advocate for Free Silver, leveraging extensive speaking tours to build a national following among "the people" against "plutocrats".
• At the 1896 Democratic National Convention, Bryan's electrifying "Cross of Gold" speech, delivered under fortuitous circumstances, galvanized the silver faction and secured his presidential nomination.
• Bryan's campaign initially adopted a divisive tone, framing the election as a class struggle and prioritizing the silver issue above all else.

McKinley's General Election Strategy: The Front-Porch Campaign

• McKinley opted for a "Front-Porch Campaign" from his Canton, Ohio home, delivering prepared, targeted remarks to visiting delegations, thus controlling his message and avoiding Bryan's frenetic pace.
• His message strategically evolved to emphasize confidence, national unity, and protection, eventually prioritizing "sound money" after initially wishing to avoid the currency issue.
• The campaign deployed unprecedented amounts of literature (over 250 million pieces) and speakers, and organized auxiliary groups to target key voter blocs like laborers, farmers, and veterans.

Key Factors in McKinley's 1896 Victory

• McKinley's victory stemmed from his focus on core issues like sound money and protection, adapting his message to voter concerns and drawing clear distinctions with Bryan.
• His campaign's message of unity and reconciliation contrasted sharply with Bryan's divisive rhetoric, further bolstered by large-scale, positive public spectacles like Flag Day parades.
• The superior organization, meticulous planning, and significant financial resources of the McKinley campaign, led by Hanna and Dawes, enabled them to effectively mobilize voters and compete across key battleground states.

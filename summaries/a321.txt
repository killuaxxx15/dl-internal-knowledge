Larry Miller (Driven: An Autobiography)

A Defining Epiphany Ignited Unstoppable Work Ethic

• At 27, with no college degree and a growing family, Miller had a moment of stark clarity: he had nothing to fall back on except his energy and talent.
• He immediately adopted a 90-hour workweek — 7:30 AM to 10–11 PM, six days a week — and sustained it for 20 years.
• His core conviction was that in a commodity business, service and hustle were the only true differentiators; he would simply outwork the competition.
• His motivation shifted over time from fear-driven to success-driven, as early results became "intoxicating."

A Dysfunctional Childhood Forged His Drive for Control

• Miller was twice taken to juvenile detention by his own mother without ever being charged with a crime, and was kicked out of the family home at 16.
• From these experiences, he resolved he would never again be subject to the whims of others — a hunger for self-determination that fueled his entire career.
• His chaotic upbringing also left him emotionally ill-equipped for fatherhood, as he had no model for what an engaged parent looked like.
• He ultimately broke a generational cycle of dysfunction, though at a great cost to his own children.

Mastering Systems and Expanding Markets Drove Explosive Growth

• Miller developed an early talent for "time and motion study" — breaking down repetitive tasks and redesigning them for maximum efficiency.
• As a parts manager, he invented a national wholesale business, expanding his market from a 10–15 mile radius to 3,000 miles, taking his dealership from 961st to 1st in the nation in 28 months.
• He was pushed into entrepreneurship after being demoted when his boss chose to hand the business to his sons — buying his first Toyota dealership in 1979 for $3.5 million with only $80,000 in the bank, leveraged at 40:1.
• Upon his death, he owned nearly 90 companies in six states — including the Utah Jazz, movie theaters, racetracks, and an NBA arena — generating $3.2 billion in annual revenue.

"Fast-Tracking" Projects: Speed as a Competitive Weapon

• When Miller built the Delta Center arena (to avoid $6–8M in annual losses staying at their old venue), he was told it would take 14–15 months just to design.
• He pioneered simultaneous design and construction — building while plans were still being drawn — completing the 20,000-seat arena in just 15 months and 24 days, the fastest major arena construction in U.S. history.
• His philosophy: things can always be done faster than conventional wisdom assumes; speed is a direct function of how seriously you take the outcome.
• He consistently refused to accept standard timelines, deadlines, or limits imposed by others.

Regret #1 — Family Neglected in Plain Sight

• Miller openly admits he was physically and emotionally absent for most of his children's lives: he missed ball games, first days of school, and family dinners; his kids learned not to knock on his office door.
• His wife Gail tried repeatedly to tell him to slow down; eventually she stopped, because "it made no difference, and it was actually easier not to have him home."
• After his death, Gail admitted: "The only time we were really together was when he got sick and could no longer go to work."
• Miller's conclusion: he would have worked 55–60 hours a week instead of 90, and been present. "Childhood does not allow itself to be reconquered."

Regret #2 — Health Destroyed by Decades of Neglect

• Miller ate poorly, never exercised after quitting softball, hid his diabetes from his own wife for years, and subjected himself to relentless stress.
• His final hospitalization lasted 59 days, during which he suffered near-fatal gastrointestinal bleeding, had both legs amputated below the knee, and lost three fingers to a rare calcium-vessel disease.
• Doctors gave him months to live on dialysis, or 5–9 days without it; he chose to go home and die surrounded by family.
• His final message: "I made things worse by eating erratically… I did not exercise and it put me on a gradual downhill slide for 23 years. The lesson, of course, is to take care of yourself. I learned that too late."
